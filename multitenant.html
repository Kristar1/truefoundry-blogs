§0 — TL;DR & Quick-Picks

🔥 Illustrative Scenario: The Midnight Embedding Job

Your ML-Ops team ships a batch pipeline overnight — 2.1 million historical customer support tickets queued for embedding via text-embedding-3-large. The pipeline runs unattended against a shared service-account key attached to your organization's AI gateway. By the time the job finishes in the small hours, it has consumed 940 million tokens, exhausting the platform's entire $300/day OpenAI budget ceiling.

At 9:00 AM, the production customer-facing support chatbot (backed by gpt-4o) begins returning HTTP 429 errors. The on-call engineer is paged. After 40 minutes of investigation, the root cause is clear: the embedding batch job and the customer-support chatbot shared the same spend envelope. There was no team-level budget isolation. There were no spending alerts targeted at the embedding workload. No circuit breaker stopped the runaway job. A single night of batch processing has made your highest-value AI product unavailable for the first six hours of the business day — right when customer success managers are trying to clear overnight ticket queues.

(This scenario is illustrative — a composite example designed to make the architecture problem concrete. The underlying failure mode it represents — teams sharing a single spend envelope with no enforced isolation — is the specific gap that team-level budgeting and rate limiting features in each gateway are designed to close.) The question every architecture review must answer before go-live: does the gateway enforce team-level isolation by policy, or does it leave that enforcement to application developers and hope they remember?

§1 — Why Multi-tenancy Is Specifically Hard for AI Gateways

Enterprise API gateways have handled multi-tenancy for years — rate-limit a consumer, scope it to a route, done. AI gateways introduce at least four new dimensions that make the problem substantially harder:

  • Spend is non-linear and difficult to predict. A single claude-opus-4-6 call with a 200k-token context can cost more than a thousand gpt-4o-mini calls. Budget segmentation must happen at the token and dollar level, not just the request-count level. A team that "only sends 50 requests/minute" can still blow through a monthly budget in hours if those requests carry large contexts.
  • The model endpoint is not a stable resource URI. A request to /v1/chat/completions can resolve to gpt-4o, claude-opus-4-6, or a self-hosted Qwen-72B depending on routing policy. URL-level ACLs are meaningless. The gateway must enforce which resolved model targets a team may access, after routing, not before it.
  • Provider credentials are a shared-tenancy blast radius. If every team consumes from the same OpenAI API key, a quota exhaustion by one team is an outage for all teams. True isolation requires each team or workload class to consume from its own spend envelope — and ideally from its own credential context.
  • Identity and workload identity are fundamentally different. Human engineers authenticate with SSO. Kubernetes pods authenticate with ServiceAccount tokens. Batch pipelines authenticate with long-lived API keys. A production AI gateway must handle all three identity surfaces simultaneously and map each to the correct team budget and policy context without requiring manual coordination.
Five dimensions of AI gateway multi-tenancy. Each vendor below addresses these with different architectural depth. Original diagram.

The sections that follow evaluate how each gateway addresses all five dimensions, with particular emphasis on what happens when different teams with different workload characteristics share the same gateway instance.

LiteLLM §2 — Virtual Key Hierarchy, K8s Workload Identity & Tag Budget Routing

Tenant Isolation Architecture

LiteLLM's multi-tenancy model is built around a three-tier hierarchy: organizations → teams → virtual keys. Each virtual key is an identity-and-policy bundle that carries a team_id, a model allowlist (models[]), and an optional personal spend cap (max_budget). When a key carries a team_id, its spend is debited from the team's budget rather than the key holder's personal budget, as documented at proxy/users. This means budget enforcement is structural, not advisory — a key assigned to the ML-Ops team cannot silently borrow from the Customer Success team's envelope.

The "Life of a Request" page at proxy/architecture documents that virtual-key validation and budget checks happen before the router — identity and economic controls precede routing decisions. This means a runaway batch job is stopped at the gate, not after it has already reached the provider.

OIDC / JWT + Kubernetes ServiceAccount Namespace Mapping

LiteLLM's JWT authentication, documented at proxy/token_auth, is one of the most operationally significant multi-tenancy features in its public docs: a single configuration field (team_alias_jwt_field) can map a Kubernetes ServiceAccount token's namespace claim directly to a LiteLLM team alias. This eliminates the "secret management for pod AI keys" problem. Pods authenticate with their cluster-native identity; the gateway resolves team membership — and therefore budget and model access — from the token.

Multiple OIDC providers are supported simultaneously via a comma-separated JWT_PUBLIC_KEY_URL environment variable, letting human engineers authenticate through the corporate IdP while K8s workloads authenticate through the cluster's OIDC issuer — both resolving to the same team-aware authorization model.

Tag-Based Budget Routing

For workload-class isolation that goes beyond organizational teams, LiteLLM offers tag budgets, documented at proxy/provider_budget_routing. Tags like "team:ml-ops" or "workload:batch-embedding" can carry their own daily spend ceilings in addition to provider-level ceilings. The router excludes exhausted targets from selection when a provider or tag budget is depleted. This is the mechanism that would have prevented the midnight embedding incident: the batch embedding tag runs out at its own daily ceiling; the production chatbot tag's ceiling remains untouched.

Per-Team Router Configuration

Routing behavior is configurable per virtual key or per team at proxy/keys_teams_router_settings. A production team's key can use latency-based routing with aggressive fallbacks, while a development team's key uses simple round-robin with no fallback. This makes the routing policy itself a multi-tenant concern, not just the budget policy.

⚠ Gap: Logical Isolation Only

LiteLLM's tenant isolation is logical. All teams share the same proxy process, the same Redis instance, and the same database. Physical compute separation — separate pods, separate network namespaces — is not a feature of the LiteLLM proxy itself. Teams that require workload-level compute isolation must achieve it outside the gateway (e.g., with separate deployments per team). The shared-process model is appropriate for most enterprise use cases but may not satisfy strict regulatory requirements for data residency or compute isolation.

📌 LiteLLM Multi-tenancy Summary

Tenant primitive: Organization → Team → Virtual Key. Model ACL: models[] allowlist per key, enforced before routing. Budget isolation: max_budget + budget_duration per team; tag budgets for workload-class isolation. Workload identity: K8s ServiceAccount JWT → team alias via team_alias_jwt_field. Gap: Logical isolation only; per-team provider credential isolation not documented in reviewed materials (operator-dependent sidecar/Vault); no SCIM provisioning documented.

Kong §3 — Consumer Group-Composed Tenant Policies

Tenant Isolation Architecture

Kong's multi-tenancy model inherits from its mature API gateway heritage: Consumers represent individual callers and Consumer Groups are the policy-bearing tenant primitive. AI-specific plugins — rate limiting, prompt guards, content safety — are attached at the Consumer Group level, meaning the same Route can serve multiple tenant classes simultaneously with completely different governance policies. As the AI audit log reference documents, from Kong 3.11 onward all AI metrics carry a consumer label, making tenant attribution first-class in observability — not a post-processing inference.

Plugin-Composed Group Policies

Each Consumer Group receives its own plugin stack. The ai-rate-limiting-advanced plugin, documented at the llm-provider-rate-limiting example, supports per-group token quotas across multiple time windows: the limit[] array and window_size[] array allow simultaneous per-minute and per-day limits on the same Consumer Group. A data science group can have a generous hourly limit but a hard daily ceiling; an external contractor group can have a much tighter per-minute cap. Both coexist on the same Service without separate routes or deployments.

Plugin-Level Vault-Backed Provider Credential Isolation

Kong's Vault integration, documented in the AI Proxy Advanced handbook section, allows provider API credentials to be referenced as {vault://aws/team-openai-key} rather than inlined. Different Consumer Groups can be routed to targets that consume different Vault-backed credentials, providing credential-level isolation between tenant classes at the proxy target layer. Raw keys never appear in declarative config or Admin API responses — a meaningful security posture for multi-tenant environments where configuration is managed by multiple operators.

Limitations of the Consumer Group Model

Consumer Group scoping is Route-and-Service–level, not metadata-key–level. Kong's AI rate-limiting plugin does not support segmentation by arbitrary request metadata fields (like metadata.project_id). A Consumer Group represents one tenant class; splitting traffic further within a group requires additional Consumers or additional Routes. For organizations with dozens of product teams that need independent limits, this can result in a large number of Consumer Groups to manage. Additionally, SCIM-based auto-provisioning from enterprise IdPs to Consumer Groups is not documented in Kong's AI Gateway public docs.

📌 Kong Multi-tenancy Summary

Tenant primitive: Consumer + Consumer Group. Model ACL: acl plugin scopes Routes to Consumer Groups. Cost-aware rate limiting: ai-rate-limiting-advanced tracks token usage and cost data per provider per Consumer Group across multi-window configurations. Credential isolation: Vault-referenced provider credentials via {vault://...} (Enterprise). Attribution: consumer label on all AI metrics from v3.11+. Gap: No metadata-key segmentation in AI plugins; no SCIM provisioning documented. Kong's rate-limiting model enforces velocity windows (tokens or cost per time window), but does not expose the same explicit cumulative-budget object with periodic_reset and alert_threshold that LiteLLM, Portkey, and TrueFoundry document natively.

Portkey §4 — Workspace-First Isolation with SCIM Auto-Provisioning

Tenant Isolation Architecture

Portkey's fundamental multi-tenancy primitive is the Workspace. Workspaces are the documented workspace-scoping boundary: the Workspaces page states that workspace API keys are scoped to the workspace by default and can only execute actions within that workspace, while the budget and rate-limit enforcement page documents workspace-level usage controls. This means a key provisioned for the ML-Ops workspace does not, per the documented model, reach configurations or budgets of the Customer Success workspace.

SCIM Auto-Provisioning (Enterprise)

Portkey documents SCIM (System for Cross-domain Identity Management) provisioning from enterprise IdPs. The SCIM pages documented at scim/okta and scim/azure-ad cover automated user provisioning and deprovisioning for Okta (via SAML app) and Microsoft Azure Entra. The SCIM Group Management guide documents two modes for mapping IdP groups to Portkey workspaces: a pattern-based naming convention and a flexible group-to-workspace mapping configured in the control plane.

This is the enterprise onboarding story that avoids manual provisioning: when a new engineer joins the ML-Ops team in Okta, they are automatically granted access to the ML-Ops workspace in Portkey and removed when they leave — no platform team ticket required.

JWT Gateway Authentication (Enterprise Add-on)

In addition to SCIM for control-plane access, Portkey documents JWT gateway authentication at the request level. Clients pass a JWT token validated against a configured JWKS (JSON Web Key Set). The JWT gateway authentication setup — including JWKS URL vs. static JWKS JSON configuration and the Admin Settings → Organisation → Authentication path — is documented at org-management/jwt. The inference API authentication page at inference-api/authentication confirms JWT+JWKS as the request-level auth mechanism. This surfaces at the gateway request level — distinct from OIDC/SAML SSO which governs dashboard login. Configured under Admin Settings → Organisation → Authentication, it supports both a dynamic JWKS URL and a static JWKS JSON payload.

Workspace Budget and Rate Limit Enforcement

The Admin SDK, documented at enforce-workspace-budget-limts-and-rate-limits, provides programmatic workspace-level spend ceilings and RPM limits. The credit_limit field with periodic_reset creates the monthly spend envelope per workspace that prevents one team's spending from affecting another. Alert thresholds generate warnings before the ceiling is hit.

Provider Credential Isolation (Enterprise)

In Enterprise deployments, Portkey's Secret References feature, documented at secret-references, stores only a reference path in the control plane while the data plane fetches the actual secret at runtime from AWS Secrets Manager, Azure Key Vault, or HashiCorp Vault (with a 5-minute cache). In the standard Model Catalog, provider keys are decrypted in-memory within sandboxed workers at the exact moment of a request. Raw credentials never appear in config objects returned by the API.

TrueFoundry §5 — K8s-Native Workspace Isolation, Two-Layer RBAC & Metadata-Scoped Limits

Tenant Isolation Architecture

TrueFoundry's multi-tenancy model is the most architecturally layered of the four vendors reviewed here, and — in this review's judgment based on the public documentation examined — the only one where tenant isolation is physically backed by Kubernetes namespace boundaries. The key-concepts page at key-concepts documents Workspaces as Kubernetes namespaces. Workspaces are the primary scoping unit: deployments, secrets, and model access are all workspace-scoped, and workspace membership combined with environment defines what a workload may access at runtime. The gateway-plane architecture page documents a stateless, in-cluster gateway plane (Hono-based) running inside the customer's K8s cluster, meaning the runtime enforcement plane sits inside the customer's network perimeter.

Two-Layer RBAC: Platform + Gateway

TrueFoundry documents two distinct RBAC layers across its public product pages. The platform-level RBAC at manage-user-roles-and-permissions governs tenant and resource permissions for configuration management. The gateway-level access control at gateway-access-control governs which subjects may access which model endpoints at runtime. This separation is architecturally deliberate: a team member can have read-only visibility to a workspace's configuration without being able to invoke model endpoints, and vice versa.

Workspaces as K8s Namespace Boundaries

Because TrueFoundry's platform runs as K8s-native infrastructure, workspace isolation maps to K8s namespace boundaries. Workloads in one workspace run in a different K8s namespace than workloads in another workspace. This means the physical compute isolation that other vendors achieve only with separate deployments is structural in TrueFoundry's model. Provider credentials live in Secret groups with FQNs (documented at manage-secrets), meaning the raw provider API key is never embedded in application manifests — it is consumed only by workloads with the appropriate workspace membership and secret group access.

Metadata-Scoped Rate Limits and Budgets

TrueFoundry's rate-limiting page at ai-gateway/ratelimiting explicitly documents rate_limit_applies_per: ['metadata.project_id'] as a supported configuration. The x-tfy-metadata request header (documented as stringified JSON with string key-value pairs) carries caller context into the gateway policy engine. This means a single static rate-limit rule can generate dynamically scoped instances — one per distinct project_id value observed in the header. An organization with 50 product teams does not need 50 separate rate-limit rules; one rule with rate_limit_applies_per: ['metadata.project_id'] creates an isolated counter per team automatically.

The same pattern applies to budgets: budget_applies_per: ['metadata.project_id'] at ai-gateway/budgetlimiting creates a dynamically-instantiated budget per project. This is in this review's judgment the most scalable metadata-based multi-tenancy control surface documented among the four vendors.

Subject-Scoped Guardrail Policies

TrueFoundry's guardrail rules support a subjects condition inside when clauses, documented at guardrails-configuration. This means content firewall policies are identity-aware: the ML-Ops team can have aggressive PII redaction and SQL injection checks on MCP tool calls, while the Data Science team gets the same PII redaction but with prompt injection detection added. Current docs say all matching guardrail rules are evaluated and the union of their guardrails is applied, so a platform team can express baseline and team-specific controls in one YAML without endpoint proliferation.

# TrueFoundry — Metadata-scoped rate limits + per-project budgets
# Source: www.truefoundry.com/docs/ai-gateway/ratelimiting
# Source: www.truefoundry.com/docs/ai-gateway/budgetlimiting
# Verified: rate_limit_applies_per with ['model'] and ['metadata.project_id']
# Verified: budget_applies_per with ['metadata.project_id']
name: org-wide-rate-limits
type: gateway-rate-limiting-config
rules:
  - id: per-project-hourly
    when: {}
    limit_to: 500000
    unit: tokens_per_hour
    rate_limit_applies_per: ['metadata.project_id']
    # One counter per distinct project_id in x-tfy-metadata header.
    # 50 teams → 50 isolated counters from one rule.
  - id: per-model-daily
    when: {}
    limit_to: 5000000
    unit: tokens_per_day
    rate_limit_applies_per: ['model']
---
name: org-wide-budgets
type: gateway-budget-config
rules:
  - id: project-daily-budget
    when: {}
    limit_to: 50
    unit: cost_per_day
    budget_applies_per: ['metadata.project_id']

§7 — Editorial Verdict

The following assessments represent editorial judgment based on documented capabilities. "Well-suited for" is the authors' characterization, not a vendor claim.

LiteLLM
The K8s-Native Workload Identity Specialist
LiteLLM's strongest differentiator for multi-tenancy is the team_alias_jwt_field mechanism — mapping K8s ServiceAccount namespace claims directly to team policy contexts. For organizations running AI workloads in Kubernetes, this eliminates a whole class of "AI key management" toil. Team budgets with max_budget and budget_duration solve the noisy-neighbor problem for dollar-denominated spend. Tag budget routing adds workload-class granularity beyond organizational teams. Gaps: logical isolation only; no SCIM provisioning; provider credential management is operator-dependent.
Well-suited for: K8s-native platform teams needing fast multi-team isolation with OIDC/workload-identity integration and budget-aware enforcement without additional infrastructure investment.
Kong AI Gateway
The Plugin-Composed Enterprise Stack
Consumer Groups inherit Kong's full API governance maturity — mTLS consumer auth (for identifying callers), OIDC, Vault-backed credential references, and the complete AI plugin catalog all compose at the group level. For organizations already running Kong, extending to AI multi-tenancy is additive rather than a new architectural commitment. Consumer identity on AI metrics from v3.11 provides attribution without custom log parsing. Gaps: no SCIM provisioning for Consumer Groups; no metadata-key segmentation in AI plugins. While Kong's ai-rate-limiting-advanced supports cost-aware rate limiting per Consumer Group, it does not expose the same cumulative-budget-ceiling object model (with periodic reset and alert thresholds) that the other three vendors document natively.
Well-suited for: Enterprises with existing Kong infrastructure needing AI multi-tenancy with the same operational model as their current API governance stack — Consumer Groups, declarative config, and Vault-backed secret references.
Portkey
The Enterprise IdP Integration Leader
Portkey's strongest differentiator in this review is its explicit public documentation for workspace-centric enterprise identity integration: SCIM auto-provisioning from Okta and Azure Entra, workspace mapping controls, JWT gateway authentication, and OIDC/SAML SSO for dashboard access. Workspaces as a documented workspace-scoping boundary with SDK-enforced budget ceilings provide a clean organizational model. Gaps: no K8s ServiceAccount JWT → team-alias mapping documented in reviewed materials; SCIM and JWT gateway auth require Enterprise plan; physical compute isolation is limited to the hybrid VPC data plane while management remains vendor-operated.
Well-suited for: Enterprise product organizations prioritizing IdP-driven workspace provisioning, spend governance, and compliance boundaries with minimal platform team operational overhead.
TrueFoundry
The Physical Isolation Platform
In this four-vendor review, TrueFoundry is the only product where tenant isolation is physically backed by Kubernetes namespace boundaries rather than being purely logical. The combination of workspace-scoped deployments, Secret group FQNs, two-layer RBAC, subject-scoped guardrail rules that union across matches, and dynamically instantiated per-project rate limits and budgets from a single static config creates the most comprehensive multi-tenancy surface reviewed. The metadata-scoped limits (rate_limit_applies_per: ['metadata.project_id']) scale gracefully from 5 teams to 500 without proportional configuration growth. Gaps: self-hosting operational burden is higher than SaaS alternatives.
Well-suited for: Regulated industries (financial services, healthcare, government), K8s-native platform teams serving many internal product teams, and organizations where logical isolation is legally insufficient.
